### Article
Twenty years behind the oar teaches you to read a river the way a musician reads sheet music—not just the notes, but the silence between them, the mood of the whole piece. Here on the Song Phraek, our guides have spent two decades learning the river's temperament, its seasonal shifts, and the subtle signs that tell you whether today will be a thrilling ride or a day better spent elsewhere.

## The Language of Water

Before any trip launches, our guides walk the riverbank at dawn. They're not just checking if the water looks "high" or "low"—they're reading a complex set of signals that most visitors wouldn't notice.

Water colour is the first indicator. After heavy rain in the mountains, the Song Phraek runs murky, carrying silt and debris downstream. This isn't just a cosmetic change; it means the water level has risen, current speed has increased, and hidden obstacles like submerged logs or boulders have shifted. A guide who has paddled this stretch hundreds of times knows that a certain shade of brown signals Grade 3 rapids becoming Grade 4, while a clear green run after dry weather means gentler currents and more predictable lines.

The sound matters too. A quiet stretch that normally babbles over rocks has gone silent—that's a blockage somewhere upstream, possibly a landslide or fallen tree. A roar where there should be a murmur means water is building behind an obstruction, ready to surge through with unexpected force. Our guides listen for these changes the way a doctor listens to a heartbeat.

## Picking the Line

White-water rafting isn't simply pointing a raft downstream and holding on. Every rapid has multiple "lines"—paths through the rocks and currents that experienced guides choose between. The wrong line can mean a bumpy ride; the right line makes the difference between an exhilarating ride and a dangerous situation.

On the Song Phraek, our guides know each boulder by name, essentially. They know that the large rock on river-left at kilometre three shifts slightly every monsoon season, meaning the safe channel moves a few metres to the right. They remember where eddy currents form—those calm pockets of water behind rocks where rafts can rest and regroup—and how to use them to control speed and direction.

The decision about which line to take depends on several factors working together: the water level, the weight distribution in the raft, the experience of the paddlers, and what lies just around the next bend. On our standard 5.5 kilometre runs, guides make dozens of micro-decisions, adjusting their plan as conditions reveal themselves moment by moment.

## When the River Says No

Sometimes the most important skill is knowing when not to paddle.

Our guides are trained to recognise conditions that cross into unsafe territory. Sustained heavy rainfall in the catchment area, visible debris floating downstream, water level markers exceeding safe thresholds—these aren't suggestions, they're hard boundaries. When these signs appear, the decision to reschedule isn't made by looking at a forecast or checking a website. It's made by someone standing on the riverbank, watching the actual water, applying two decades of lived experience.

This is where our cancellation policy becomes relevant. We promise guests a full refund or free reschedule if we cancel for weather or safety reasons—not because we're being overly cautious, but because we've seen what happens when experience is ignored. The river will always be there tomorrow. Our guides' job is to make sure our guests are too.

When we do reschedule, it's never a hassle. Guests simply rebook for another day, and because we keep our group sizes small—between two and ten people—we usually find availability quickly. The balance paid on the day is simply transferred to the new booking.

## FAQ

### What happens if it's raining on the day of my booking?

Rain in Phuket doesn't automatically cancel a trip—our guides assess the actual river conditions. If the rainfall has affected the Song Phraek's water level or created unsafe conditions, we'll contact you before pickup to reschedule or offer a full refund.

### Can I request a specific guide?

We don't take reservations for specific guides, but if you've paddled with us before and have a favourite, let our team know when you book. We'll do our best to accommodate requests, though guide assignments depend on scheduling.

### What should I bring on a rafting trip?

Wear comfortable clothing that can get wet, secure footwear with grippy soles, and bring a change of clothes for afterwards. We'll provide life jackets, paddles, and all necessary safety equipment. Avoid valuables that shouldn't get wet.

### How do I know if river conditions are right for my skill level?

Our tours range from standard 5.5 kilometre runs to extended 7.5 kilometre routes. When you book, tell us about your experience and comfort level—our team will recommend the package that matches your group, and guides will adjust the intensity of the run based on conditions and participants.
